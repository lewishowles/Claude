# Writing style

## Voice & tone

- **First person, personal** — use "I" and "we" naturally. Share relevant personal observations or experience when it adds credibility ("This was an interesting one when it dawned on me")
- **Empathetic, not prescriptive** — lead with understanding, not rules. "We should try to think about..." not "You must always..."
- **Conversational** — write as you'd explain it to a colleague, not as a policy document
- **Honest** — acknowledge complexity ("There's quite a lot in here") rather than oversimplifying
- **Inclusive** — use "we" for shared actions. Humanise subjects; refer to real scenarios and real people, not abstract users

## Structure

- **Why before rules** — explain the problem or motivation before stating the solution or guideline
- **Concrete before abstract** — ground concepts in a real scenario before generalising
- **Short before long** — lead with the point, then expand. Don't bury the key idea in paragraph three
- **Analogies welcome** — use everyday comparisons to make unfamiliar concepts land ("just like I'd expect a solicitor to handle the fundamentals of law")

## Examples & evidence

- Provide before/after examples for anything where "better" is subjective
- Use statistics when they exist and support the point — cite the source
- Scenarios > rules — a concrete "imagine you're on a pricing page..." beats "always provide confirmation"
- Humanise examples — "Lewis Howles" not "User X"

## Language

- **UK spelling** — colour, behaviour, organise, grey, recognise, etc.
- **Sentence case titles** — not Title Case
- **Plain language** — if someone unfamiliar with the topic can't follow it, simplify. No jargon without explanation
- **Contractions** — fine; they keep the tone natural
- **Italics** for emphasis; `backticks` for inline code, technical terms, and UI strings
- **Em dash** for parenthetical asides — no spaces around it
- **No preamble or summary** unless specifically asked

## What to avoid

- Preachy or lecturing tone — state the point once; don't repeat it as a moral
- Padding ("It's worth noting that...", "As mentioned above...")
- Prescriptive absolutes where context matters — "generally", "ideally", "where possible" are honest
- Capitalising every word in titles or headings
- Opening with a summary of what you're about to say
